### CLASSIFICATION RULES (Revised and More Flexible) ###
**1. Behaviourally Cautious:** Classify here if the argument's **primary thrust** is one of the following:
- **Emotional Framing:** Uses fear-based or gambling-related language ("scary," "casino," "wipeout," "get rich quick").
- **Simplistic Heuristics:** Uses a concept like "volatility decay" or "drawdowns" as an absolute, out-of-context "knock-out" argument to dismiss the strategy entirely, ignoring long-term data.
- **Logical Fallacies:** Relies on appeals to authority without evidence ("the prospectus says so") or absolutist claims ("you will ALWAYS lose money").
 
**2. Rationally Cautious:** Classify here if the argument's **primary thrust** is one of the following:
- **Personal Risk Tolerance:** Acknowledges that the volatility, while potentially rewarded, is too high for their personal comfort or financial situation.
- **Historical Uncertainty:** Cites the limited lifespan of the products or specific historical periods (e.g., the dot-com bust) as a logical reason for skepticism about future performance.
- **Nuanced Analysis:** Presents a balanced, data-aware argument that acknowledges both pros and cons, even if the final conclusion is negative.
 
**3. Behaviourally Positive:** Classify here if the argument's **primary thrust** is one of the following:
- **Hype or Emotion:** Relies on excitement, slogans ("to the moon!"), or pure conviction without providing a logical or strategic basis.
- **Overconfidence:** Makes bold, unsubstantiated claims about future performance without acknowledging risks.
 
**4. Rationally Positive:** Classify here if the argument's **primary thrust** is one of the following:
- **Strategic Reasoning:** Focuses on methods for managing risk, such as specific rebalancing rules, diversification, hedging, or having a long time horizon.
- **Data-Driven Support:** Uses backtested data, historical examples, or logical reasoning to justify a positive long-term outlook while still acknowledging the risks involved.
